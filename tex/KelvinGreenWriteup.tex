\begin{abstract}
Linear wave theory captures the essential physics of free-surface flows at a fraction of the computational cost of nonlinear and viscous methods, making it attractive for applications in design, real-time control, and surrogate modeling. However, linear wave predictions for ships with forward speed require evaluating the Kelvin Green's function at the free surface $z=0$, where the point-source kernel is ill-posed; the wave elevation grows without bound as $z\to 0^-$ even far downstream, causing both numerical and physical interpretation difficulties. This paper develops a modified kernel for flat-ship theory incorporating elliptic spanwise line integration that naturally resolves this ill-posedness, yielding finite wave energy over the entire free surface. We then present a fast evaluator for both point and line kernels using contour deformation adapted to the non-analytic Kelvin phase, achieving $10^4$-$10^5$ speedup over direct quadrature while preserving far-wake asymptotics. An open-source Julia implementation is provided.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Linear potential theory for steady forward-speed wave problems captures the essential physics of ship-wave interaction at a tiny fraction of the computational cost of nonlinear and viscous solvers. This speed makes it attractive for applications such as design optimization, real-time control, and as the physics-informed backbone for machine learning surrogate models.  However, the predictions also need to be free from empirical tuning parameters and robust for any input provided, which can be a problem for undamped potential flow.

The Kelvin Green's function for a steady translating disturbance exactly satisfies the linear free-surface boundary condition and has a well-established integral representation (Peters 1949; Noblesse 1981). For finite submergence, analytic and surrogate methods exist (Farrell 1973; Newman 1987), but the elevation of the free surface is ill-posed as the source position moves up to the free surface, i.e., as $z\to 0^-$. The wave elevation spectrum $S_\zeta(k;z)$ peaks at $k^* \sim 1/|z|$ with amplitude $\sim 1/|z|$, diverging as $|z|\to 0^-$ and making the downstream wake unresolvable. Series and steepest-descent methods (Baar and Price 1988; Iwashita and Ohkusu 1992) struggle with convergence, with stationary point coalescence, and with high wavenumbers in this limit. Recent contour deformation approaches (Huybrechs 2017; Gibbs \& Huybrechs 2024) are effective for analytic oscillatory integrands, but the Kelvin wavelike integral is non-analytic.

For surface-piercing bodies, the free-surface limit becomes unavoidable. The waterline contour contributes directly to the potential and must be evaluated at $z=0$, (Baar and Price 1988). Classic flat ship theory (Tuck, Doctors, etc.) prioritized practical prediction of wave resistance and hull pressure for high-speed, shallow-draft vessels, often using analytic or semi-analytic models and empirical corrections (e.g., spray) to match experiments. However, the singularity in the free-surface Green's function at $z\to 0^-$ was not fully resolved, and direct evaluation of the waterline contribution remains problematic. The persistent difficulty of evaluating the free-surface Green's function led to the development of the Neumann-Michell theory (Noblesse, Huang, Yang 2013), which avoids evaluation on $z=0$ using an implicit iterative scheme. Notably, Neumann-Michell theory was developed after the classic flat ship work, highlighting that the fundamental issues remain unresolved. This method sidesteps the $z\to 0^-$ singularity but sacrifices the directness and efficiency of explicit kernel representations.

This paper addresses the $z\to 0^-$ limit with an emphasis on obtaining meaningful finite energy free-surface predictions. The first contribution is analytical: Section~\ref{sec:point_source_asymptotics} identifies the limiting wave elevation spectrum $S_\zeta(k^*;z)\sim 1/|z|$ stated above via stationary-phase analysis; Section~\ref{sec:line_integrated_kernels} then develops a modified flat-ship model for $z\approx 0$, deriving the elliptic spanwise distribution of source strength that arises from the constant-downwash condition. The leading order flat-ship potential \textit{is} the waterline contribution, but analytic integration replaces the divergent spectrum with a finite $S_\zeta(k;0)\sim 1/k^{3/2}$. The second contribution is numerical: Section~\ref{sec:fast_evaluator} develops a practical evaluator for both point and line kernels using contour deformation adapted to the non-analytic Kelvin phase, achieving $10^4$--$10^5$ speedup over direct quadrature while preserving the wave energy. The flat-ship theory, requiring only two (leading and trailing edge) kernel evaluations per field point, is then used to study the wave elevation and wave drag generated in the $z=0$ limit by a rectangular planform as a function of the length and beam based Froude-number. An open-source Julia implementation is provided for reproducibility.


\section{Kelvin Green's Function and Waterline Integral}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{flat_ship_schematic.svg}
    \caption{Schematic of the flat ship model showing rectangular planform of length $L$ and half-beam $b$ at the free surface $z=0$, with coordinate system, flow direction $U$, angle of attack $\alpha$, and Kelvin wedge.}
    \label{fig:schematic}
\end{figure}

Consider an inviscid, incompressible, irrotational flow in the half-space $Z<0$. The velocity potential $\Phi$ satisfies Laplace's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 0$ with the linearized free-surface boundary condition for steady forward speed $U$
\[
\ell\,\Phi_{XX} + \Phi_Z = 0\quad\text{on}\quad Z=0
\]
where $\ell=U^2/g$ is the Kelvin length. Scaling lengths by $\ell$ and the potential by $U\ell$, the Kelvin Green's function of a translating point source at $\vec x_p$, satisfying the FSBC by construction, is
\[
G\bigl(\vec x,\vec x_p\bigr) = N(\vec x,\vec x_p) + W(\vec x - \vec x_p')
\]
where $\vec x_p'$ is the image of $\vec x_p$ reflected across $z=0$, and $N$, $W$ are the near-field and wavelike terms. We have combined all of the near-field terms together, as in Noblesse et al. 2013, and fast surrogates are known for their efficient evaluation, as in Newman 1987. 

In this work we focus on the wavelike term
\[
W(\vec x) = 4H(-x)\int_{-\infty}^\infty \exp\bigl((1+t^2)z\bigr)\sin\bigl((x+yt)\sqrt{1+t^2}\bigr)\di t
\]
where $H$ is the Heaviside function confining the wake downstream and $t=\tan\theta$ and $\theta$ is the wave direction relative to $x$. The induced potential of a source-patch area $\mathcal{C}$ is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:patch}
\phi(\vec x) = \iint_{\mathcal{C}} q(\vec x_p)\,G(\vec x,\vec x_p)\,\di{a_p}    
\end{equation}
where $\phi = \Phi/U\ell$ and $q$ is the $U$-scaled source strength. If $\mathcal{C}$ lies near or on $z=0$, evaluating the free-surface elevation $\zeta = \partial_x\phi|_{z=0}$ and wave drag require evaluating $G$, and in particular $W$, close to $z=0$. We show in the next section that this limit is singular for a point source. This divergence is well known in practice, spurring developments such as Neumann-Michell theory, but we characterize it explicitly below as a basis for comparison.

\section{Point-Source Singularity as $z\to 0^-$}\label{sec:point_source_asymptotics}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.55\linewidth]{make_figures/wavecut.png}
    \includegraphics[width=0.44\linewidth]{make_figures/spectrum.png}
    \caption{Free surface wave cut and spectra downstream of a unit point-source with $z\to 0^-$ and line-integrated source on $z=0$. The wave cuts on the left are $\zeta$ evaluated at $x=-8$. The spectra on the right are $S^*_\zeta=|\zeta|^2$ evaluated at $x=-40$ along the divergent wave ridge $y(t_+)$, equation~\ref{eq:sp}, with the carrier demodulated via Hilbert transform. The line-integrated results use half-beam $b=1$ and $q_0=\frac{2}{\pi}$.}
    \label{fig:wavecut}
\end{figure}

We write the wavelike term in the general amplitude form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wavelike_general}
W_A(x,y,z) = 4H(-x)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} A(t)\,\exp\bigl(z(1+t^2)\bigr)\,\sin\bigl(g(x,y,t)\bigr)\,\di t
\end{equation}
with phase $g = (x+yt)\sqrt{1+t^2}$. For a point-source we have $A\equiv 1$, but this form anticipates the analytic integration of Section~\ref{sec:line_integrated_kernels}. 

Stationary points of the wavelike phase satisfy $\partial_t g = 0$, and are given by 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sp}
t_\pm = -\frac{x \pm \sqrt{x^2 - 8y^2}}{4y}
\end{equation}
corresponding to the transverse and diverging wave systems inside the Kelvin wedge $|y| < |x|/\sqrt{8}$. On the diverging ridge near the centerline, $t_+ \approx -x/(2y) \to \infty$ as $y\to 0$. A standard stationary-phase estimate for the diverging wave contribution on $z=0$ gives
\[
W(x,y,0) \sim \left(\frac{t_+}{R}\right)^{1/2},\qquad R = |\vec x|
\]
which grows without bound as $t_+\to\infty$. 

For finite point source submergence $z_p<0$, the diverging wave contribution to the wavelike elevation spectrum $S_\zeta = |\partial_x W|^2$ is 
\begin{equation}
  S_\zeta(k;\,z_p) \sim \frac{k}{R}\,\exp\left(-2|z_p|k\right),
  \label{eq:point_source_spectrum}
\end{equation}
peaking at
\begin{equation}
  k^* = \frac{1}{2|z_p|}, \qquad S_\zeta(k^*;\,z) \sim \frac{1}{R\,|z_p|}.
  \label{eq:point_source_peak}
\end{equation}
Therefore, the exponential $\exp(-|z_p|t_+^2)$ provides damping, but as $|z_p|\to 0$ the peak still migrates to arbitrarily high wavenumber with unbounded amplitude. This is not a local artifact: every point along the downstream wake is affected, with amplitude only slowly modulated with $R^{-1}$. Figure~\ref{fig:wavecut} illustrates the unbounded growth as $z_p\to 0^-$ well downstream, in contrast with the regularized line-integrated kernel derived in the next section.

\section{Line-Integrated Potential and Flat-Ship Theory}\label{sec:line_integrated_kernels}

The singularity identified above motivates replacing the point-source waterline kernel with an integrated Green's function. The flat-ship model provides a uniquely challenging setting since the entire surface rests on $z=0$!

\subsection{The Flat-Ship Model}\label{ssec:flat_ship_model}

Consider a surface-piercing horizontal planform at small angle of attack $\alpha$ such as a flat-bottom planing hull. The flat-ship boundary condition imposes uniform downwash on the planform
\[
\partial_n\phi = \alpha, \qquad (x_p,y_p)\in\mathcal{C},\quad z_p\to 0^-.
\]
In the limit $z_p\to 0^-$, the surface integral that defines the potential, equation~\ref{eq:patch} reduces to a waterline contour integral around the planform boundary $\partial\mathcal{C}$ (Noblesse 1983)
\[
\phi(\vec x) = \oint_{\partial\mathcal{C}} q(\vec x_p)\,G(\vec x-\vec x_p)\,n_x\,\di{y_p}.    
\]
For illustration, we focus on a rectangular planform of half-beam $b$ and length $L$, the contour $\partial\mathcal{C}$ consists of the leading and trailing edges, and since $n_x = \pm 1$ on these edges the potential reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fs pot1}
\phi(\vec x) = \int_{-b}^{b} q(y_p)\Delta_L G(x,y-y_p,z)\di{y_p}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_L G(x) = G(x)-G(x-L)$ is the difference across the bow and stern.

The remaining unknown is the spanwise distribution $q(y_p)$. To determine it, we enforce the downwash condition on the planform. Substituting $G = N + W$ and noting that in the near field $W$ slowly varies in $y$ compared to $N$, the dominant operator acting on $q$ is the logarithmic near field term:
\[
\int_{-b}^{b} q(y_p)\log|y-y_p|\,\di{y_p} = \mathrm{const},\qquad |y|<b.
\]
This is precisely the classical constant-downwash integral equation of finite-wing theory, whose unique solution on $[-b,b]$ is the elliptic distribution
\[
q(y_p) = q_0\sqrt{1-(y_p/b)^2}.
\]
Using this elliptic distribution satisfies the downwash condition to leading order, neglecting the wavelike influence on the spanwise loading as in classical thin-ship theory. As shown in the following, it also admits an exact integral representation that regularizes the kernel for all $z$.

\subsection{The Line-Integrated Wavelike Kernel}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.495\linewidth]{make_figures/wave_field_b1.png}
    \includegraphics[width=0.495\linewidth]{make_figures/wave_field_b2.png}
    \caption{Flat-ship wave elevation $\zeta$ scaled by the spanwise integrated strength $\tfrac \pi 2 q_0b$ for $L=5$ and $b=1,\, 2$ on the left and right, respectively.}
    \label{fig:wave_elevation}
\end{figure}

We define the elliptically weighted spanwise integral of $W$ as
%
\begin{align}
  W_b(x,y,z) &= \int_{-b}^{b} \sqrt{1-(y_p/b)^2}\,W(x,y-y_p,z)\, \di{y_p} \notag\\
  &=  4H(-x)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(z(1+t^2)\right) \int_{-b}^{b} \sqrt{1-\left(\frac{y_p}{b}\right)^2} \,\sin\bigl(g(x,y-y_p,t)\bigr)\,\di{y_p}\,\di t
  \label{eq:Wb_def}
\end{align}
%
where we have swapped the order of integration. The inner integral is the Fourier transform of the elliptic distribution, which has the exact Bessel function representation 
\[
\int_{-1}^1 \sqrt{1-\eta^2} e^{i\omega\eta} \di\eta= \pi \frac{J_1(\omega)}\omega
\]
where $\mathrm{J}_1$ is the Bessel function of the first kind. Substituting $\eta=y_p/b$ and $\omega=bk_y$, where $k_y(t)=t\sqrt{1+t^2}$, $W_b$ takes the general form~\eqref{eq:wavelike_general} with amplitude $A_b$
\begin{equation}
  A_b(t) = \pi\,\frac{\mathrm{J}_1\bigl(b\,k_y(t)\bigr)}{k_y(t)}.
  \label{eq:Ab}
\end{equation}
%
The factor $A_b$ is finite at $k_y=0$ (with limit $\frac 12 \pi b$) and decays as $k_y^{-3/2}$. Applying the stationary-phase analysis of Section~\ref{sec:point_source_asymptotics} with amplitude $A_b$ at $z=0$ gives the wave elevation spectrum
%
\begin{equation}
  S_{b,\zeta}(k;\,0) \sim \frac{\mathrm{J}_1^2\left(b\,k_y\right)}{k_y^2}\frac{k_y^{3/2}}{R} \sim \frac{1}{k_y^{3/2}bR},
  \label{eq:Sb_spectrum}
\end{equation}
%
which is uniformly decaying and integrable, completely eliminating the unbounded high-wavenumber energy of the point-source kernel. This is demonstrated in Figure~\ref{fig:wavecut} for $b=1$.

Using this, the integrated flat-ship potential of equation~\ref{eq:fs pot1} becomes
\[  
\phi(\vec x)/q_0 = \Delta_L W_b(x,y,z)+\int_{-b}^b \sqrt{1-(y_p/b)^2}\, \Delta_L N(x,y-y_p,z)\,\di{y_p}
\]
where the near-field term $N$ can be integrated numerically without issue and the wavelike term is calculated using the partitioned contour deformation method described in Section~\ref{sec:fast_evaluator}.

A set of resulting flat-ship wave-fields are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:wave_elevation} for various $b,L$. First, we note that the predicted wave-elevations are finite amplitude and energy everywhere despite evaluation on $z=0$. Second, the predictions capture many well-known physical patterns beyond the obvious Kelvin-wedge and wake shape. In particular, directly behind the planform we see the interaction of the stern corner waves generates a large rooster-tail in the near wake, after which the wave trains diverge leaving a rough but low amplitude wave-field in the far wake. The distinct wave trains generated by each corner of the planform also imply that the use of a smooth elliptical distribution has not artificially \textit{rounded} the geometry.

An initially counter-intuitive feature is the strong \textit{decrease} in wave amplitude with increasing $b$. However, this is resolved by acknowledging the Kelvin scaling applied to our lengths; as we increase $b$, it becomes too long to strongly interact with the Kelvin wavelengths $\lambda=2\pi\ell\cos^2\theta$. In terms of Froude numbers, we have $\mathrm{F_b}=b^{-1/2}$, and $\mathrm{F_L}=L^{-1/2}$; increasing $b,L$ decreases the Froude numbers, typically decreasing the wave disturbance amplitude.

\subsection{Wave Resistance}
\label{ssec:wave_resistance}
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{make_figures/wave_drag.png}
    \caption{Flat ship wave drag coefficient $C_W$ scaled by $q_0^2$ as a function of the Kelvin-scaled planform length $L$ and width $b$.}
    \label{fig:wave_drag}
\end{figure}

The flat-ship wave resistance $D_W$ follows from the Havelock formula. Working in the variable $t = \tan\theta$ and using $k_x(t)= \sqrt{1+t^2}=\sec\theta$ we have 
%
\begin{equation}
  D_W = \frac{\rho}{\pi\ell^2} \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} |\mathrm{K}(\theta)|^2\,\sec^3\theta\,\di\theta = \frac{\rho}{\pi\ell^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\mathrm{K}(t)|^2\, k_x(t)\,\di t
  \label{eq:havelock}
\end{equation}
%
where the Kochin function $\mathrm{K}(t)$ is the source-strength-weighted integral of the wavelike kernel over the planform. For the elliptic spanwise distribution and rectangular planform,
\[
\mathrm{K}(t) = q_0\,A_b(t)\Delta_Le^{ixk_x}.
\]
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:havelock} and defining $C_W = D_W/(\rho U^2 b^2\ell^2)$ gives
%
\begin{equation}
  C_W = 2\pi q_0^2 \int_0^{\infty}
  A_w\bigl(1 - \cos\bigl(Lk_x\bigr)\bigr)\,\di t,\qquad A_w=\left(\frac{\mathrm{J}_1(b\,k_y)}{bk_y}\right)^2 k_x,
  \label{eq:Cw}
\end{equation}
%
with $q_0 \propto \alpha$. The amplitude $A_w$ is $O(1)$ at $t=0$ and decays as $t^{-5}$ for large $t$, so~\eqref{eq:Cw} is well-posed and rapidly convergent. 

Figure~\ref{fig:wave_drag} plots scaled resistance across a wide range of $b,L$. First, we note that the interference factor $1-\cos(Lk_x)$ produces classical constructive and destructive interference between the bow and stern wave systems, resulting in oscillating resistance values with $L$. Second, the strong decay of the $A_w$ weighting with increased $b$ leads to a monotonic decrease in scaled resistance. This is also physically supported by the discussion on the wave elevation of the previous subsection. 

\section{Partitioned evaluation method for wavelike kernels}\label{sec:fast_evaluator}

Direct numerical quadrature and series expansions for the wavelike integral equation~\ref{eq:wavelike_general} are extremely inefficient as $z\to0$. In this section, we detail the efficient and universally robust partitioned contour-deformation approach used to generate the results of the previous sections. An open-source Julia implementation is provided.

\subsection{Defining the saddle regions and quadrature methods}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.495\linewidth]{make_figures/dphase_z.png}
    \includegraphics[width=0.495\linewidth]{make_figures/dphase_GLn.png}
    \caption{Error convergence of the partitioned quadrature method applied to $W(-8,y,z)$ on the same wavecuts in figure \ref{fig:wavecut}. Left shows the independence of the error as $z\to 0^-$ for $N=4$ Gauss-Laguerre points, and right shows the reduction of error with increasing $N$ for $z=-0.01$.}
    \label{fig:delta_phase}
\end{figure}

As in Gibbs and Huybrechs 2023, the integral is partitioned into non-oscillatory intervals around stationary points, where standard quadrature is efficient, and the remaining semi-infinite tails which are deformed into the complex plane, where the integrand decays exponentially. Unlike the analytic phase functions treated in Gibbs and Huybrechs, the wavelike phase $g(x,y,t) = (x+yt)\sqrt{1+t^2}$ is non-analytic and introduces branch points at $t = \pm i$. Therefore, we limit the non-oscillatory intervals to the real axis, defined such that each endpoint is separated from the nearest stationary point by a prescribed phase increment $\Delta g$. Limiting the intervals to the real axis also simplifies locating the partition boundaries with numerical 1D root-finding.

The kernel is smooth over the non-oscillatory interval by construction, meaning Gauss-Legendre quadrature with a fixed number of points related to $\Delta g$ can be applied. For this manuscript, we instead use adaptive Gauss-Kronrod quadrature to ensure a prescribed accuracy. The semi-infinite tails are evaluated using numerical steepest descent on a complex path emanating from the interval endpoints using Gauss-Laguerre quadrature appropriate for exponentially decaying kernels. The contour points are located by Newton's method, selecting the $\sqrt{1+t^2}$ branch such that the phase remains continuous along each contour. Outside the Kelvin wedge, a single pseudo-stationary point $t_0=-x/4y=\Re(t_\pm)$ is used, and the procedure is otherwise identical; this ensures continuity across the wedge boundary and the correct exponential decay in its vicinity.

Figure~\ref{fig:delta_phase} shows the maximum error of the point-source wavelike integral using this partitioning method. The error envelope is insensitive to $\vec x$, handling the wave cusp merge as $y\to \pm x/\sqrt{8}$ and the energy growth as $z\to 0^-$ gracefully. The phase spacing $\Delta g$ is the primary numerical parameter of the approach, encoding a tradeoff between the number of oscillations integrated along the real line and the exponential decay along the contours. If the contour starts too close to a stationary point (small $\Delta g$), the phase is slowly varying and the integrand decays slowly in the complex plane, requiring more Gauss-Laguerre points for accuracy. Conversely, large $\Delta g$ ensures rapid decay and efficient contour quadrature, but increases the oscillations in the real-axis intervals, making them more expensive to integrate. The number of Gauss-Laguerre points $N$, is a secondary parameter: increasing $N$ increases the error decay rate but also increases the length of the contours and computational expense. Herein, we set $\Delta g \approx 6$ and $N=4$, producing maximum errors around $10^{-6}$ with minimal computational cost.

The same contour-deformation strategy applies to the line-integrated kernel, extended to handle the additional oscillatory structure introduced by the Bessel prefactor in equation~\ref{eq:Ab}. The Bessel function is decomposed using Hankel functions away from $t = 0$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Hankel}
\mathrm{J}_1(bk_y) = \frac{1}{2}\left(\mathrm{Hx}_1^{(1)}(bk_y)\,e^{ibk_y} + \mathrm{Hx}_1^{(2)}(bk_y)\,e^{-ibk_y}\right)
\end{equation}
where the scaled Hankel functions $\mathrm{Hx}_1^{(1,2)}$ are slowly varying. The exponential factors $e^{\pm i b\,k_y(t)}$ introduce additional oscillations whose frequency depends on the beam half-width $b$, but these can be absorbed into the wavelike phase, yielding two integrals with shifted phases $g(x,y \pm b,t)$. Each shifted phase has its own stationary points and associated non-oscillatory interval; the union of these intervals is used on the real line, ensuring that the contour starting points are sufficiently far from any stationary point. This interval is integrated (once) using the $A_b$ prefactor, while the semi-infinite tails are computed twice, once for each Hankel function using its respective phase.\footnote{The same approach can also be applied to the wave resistance integral~\eqref{eq:Cw}. However, the faster $A_w\sim t^{-5}$ decay and slower $k_x\sim t$ phase function mean direct quadrature is also practical for that integral.}

In general, the $W_b$ quadrature requires approximately twice as many phase and kernel evaluations as $W$, and Bessel function evaluations are $O(10)$ times as expensive as simple trigonometric functions. However, the computational cost is still $10^4$--$10^5$ faster than direct quadrature on $z=0$ with relative errors less than $10^{-6}$, Table~\ref{tab:wavelike}.

\section{Conclusions}

The singularity in the point-source Kelvin Green's function at $z=0$ is fundamental: the wave elevation spectrum diverges as $S_\zeta(k^*;z)\sim 1/|z|$ with unbounded high-wavenumber energy. However, this singularity is not intrinsic to the flat-ship problem itself. The constant-downwash boundary condition naturally produces an elliptic spanwise source distribution, and analytic integration of this distribution yields a regularized kernel with finite spectrum $S_\zeta(k;0)\sim k^{-3/2}$. The waterline evaluation is therefore well-posed when the correct source distribution is used.

This resolution enables practical flat-ship predictions without empirical damping or iterative methods. The resulting predictions are physically consistent, capturing wave interference patterns and realistic Froude number trends. The contour-deformation approach developed here evaluates both point and line kernels efficiently ($10^4$--$10^5$ speedup) despite the non-analytic Kelvin phase, making the method suitable for design and machine learning applications. 

We note that the elliptic distribution developed here is merely the leading-order solution for flat planforms. Accounting for local hull geometry while retaining the regularized kernel framework is a natural extension. The fast evaluation method also applies to time-domain problems and could be coupled with lifting-surface theory for control surfaces.
